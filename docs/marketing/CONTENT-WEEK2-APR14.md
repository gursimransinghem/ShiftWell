### Slide 2 — Adenosine: The Sleep Pressure Signal

**Headline:** Why caffeine "works" — and why it backfires.

**Body:** Adenosine is a chemical that builds up in your brain while you're awake, creating sleep pressure. The longer you're awake, the more adenosine accumulates — and the sleepier you feel. Caffeine doesn't give you energy. It blocks adenosine receptors. You're still building sleep pressure. You're just temporarily muting the signal.

*(Visual: Simple diagram — adenosine builds up over wakefulness, caffeine "blocks" the signal)*

---

### Slide 3 — The Half-Life Problem

**Headline:** Caffeine has a 5-6 hour half-life. Plan accordingly.

**Body:** If you drink a 200mg coffee at 6 PM, 100mg of caffeine is still in your system at midnight. 50mg is still there at 6 AM. For shift workers whose sleep window starts mid-morning or early afternoon, late-night caffeine creates a direct collision with the sleep window you need most.

*(Visual: Timeline graph showing caffeine level decay across a 12-hour period. Highlight "danger zone" overlap with sleep window.)*

---

### Slide 4 — Circadian Timing Changes Everything

**Headline:** Your circadian clock affects how caffeine hits.

**Body:** Caffeine sensitivity varies across your circadian cycle. In the biological morning (shortly after your core body temperature starts rising), caffeine is most effective. In the "circadian trough" — typically mid-afternoon for day workers, but shifted for night workers — caffeine feels weaker. Drinking more to compensate extends the window it stays in your system.

*(Visual: Circadian curve with labeled "effective zone" and "danger zone" for caffeine timing.)*

---

### Slide 5 — Night Shift Cutoff Rules

**Headline:** Night shift? Your caffeine cutoff is not midnight.

**Body:** If your sleep window begins at 8 AM after a night shift, your caffeine cutoff is approximately 2-3 AM — not later. Using caffeine after 3 AM on a night shift compresses your recovery sleep and fragments your REM, even if you fall asleep. The crash you feel the next "morning" is often accumulated adenosine that finally won — plus a shortened, degraded sleep cycle.

**Science note:** Based on caffeine half-life data and circadian adenosine kinetics (Drake et al., 2013).

*(Visual: Night shift timeline — shift hours, caffeine cutoff window highlighted in amber/orange)*

### Slide 6 — Rotating Shifts: The Moving Target

**Headline:** Rotating shifts? Your cutoff time changes with every rotation.

**Body:** The hardest part about caffeine timing for rotating shift workers isn't discipline — it's that the optimal cutoff time is *different* for every shift type. Day shift, evening shift, night shift, and 24-hour on-call all have different sleep windows, which means different caffeine cutoffs. Memorizing one rule won't work. You need a plan that changes when your schedule does.
